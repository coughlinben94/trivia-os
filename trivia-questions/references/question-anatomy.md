# Question Anatomy — Deep Style Reference

This is the style core. Every example below is a real Ben question (light cleanup only). Read this whole file before writing a batch of questions. The goal is not to imitate the *topics* — it's to internalize the *moves*.

## 1. The Two-Clue Lattice, Dissected

The signature. Two (or more) independent routes to one answer, usually crossing domains. Teams solve from EITHER side; the table conversation is the two sides meeting.

**Route A (history) × Route B (comics):**
> The world's first programmable digital computer was invented in 1943 Britain and was used to decode German ciphers. It shares its name with the only original 15 X-Men that was from mainland Asia. What was the name of this computer? → **Colossus**

**Route A (etymology) × Route B (basketball resume joke):**
> What was the archaic name for someone who prepared and sold medicine to physicians and patients in the 13th and 14th century? Deriving from a term describing a place where wine and spices are stored, this term may also show up on the resume of one D Rose. → **Apothecary**
*(Note the bridge is delivered as a joke — "Derrick Rose = D Rose = the rapper's pharmacy line" is left for the table to unpack. Bridges can be riddles.)*

**Three-route lattice (song × geography × Marvel):**
> British singer George Ezra had a 2014 chart topping song that chronicled him being far from what city, in which he was looking for love, where one may find Hawkeye and Black Widow? → **Budapest**

**The "shares its name with" workhorse** — the most common bridge connector. Variants Ben rotates: *shares its name with / can also be found in / lends its name to / takes its name from / you could ask [character] / [character] can probably tell you.* Rotate them; never use the same connector twice in one round.

**Character-as-witness bridge** (framing a clue as a person who'd know):
> Louisiana is the only state in the country that uses what term instead of county for judicial districts? You can ask the home run leader and everyday battery mate from the 1984 Tigers; he may be from there… → **Parish** (Lance Parrish)
*(The witness IS the pun. This is a beloved move — the bridge clue works only when said aloud.)*

## 2. Hook Taxonomy (cold opens)

Hooks precede the question, unattributed, unexplained. Four species:

**A. The secret third clue** — a quote/sound whose SOURCE is the answer or adjacent to it:
> "Ow! My Patella!" → question about the last Indian Wars conflict → **Wounded Knee** (knee joke)
> "*FLASH*" → Men in Black question
> "Leedle Leedle Leedle Lee!" → **Flying Dutchman** (SpongeBob's Dutchman)
> "Stayin alive, stayin alive!" → **Pulsar** (a pulse)
> "I want to touch it" → anglerfish → **Finding Nemo** (Dory's line)

**B. The tonal misdirect** — a quote from a property that shares a WORD with the answer, not the topic:
> "Judge me by my ability, do you?" (Yoda) → Death Star question. Related property, different corner.
> "Elphaba never sleeps!" → "Ain't No Rest for the Wicked" (wicked).

**C. The pure bit** — comedy, no clue: "Your momma is so old, her breast milk is powdered!" (→ Tiffany's silver purity). "It's giving birth!" (→ Pandemonium, Paradise Lost). These earn their place by rhythm alone.

**D. The venue/topical open** — "There is a great disturbance here at Baynes trivia… last week the acting world lost…" / "With JV being back, we must start with a baseball question…" — grounds the show in the room and the week.

Ratio guidance: roughly 40% of standalone questions carry a hook; within a round, vary the species. Never two type-A hooks back to back.

## 3. Trailing Wink Taxonomy

The ellipsis-tailed aside AFTER the question mark. Three species:

**A. The extra sideways clue:** "Don't go too fast, though, we wouldn't want you to turn to plaid…" (Spaceballs → **Ludicrous** speed). "No wonder they're so happy…" (→ **Finnish**/finish, happiest country).
**B. The local/personal tag:** "…he has two locations in Saginaw, two in Bay City, and one in Midland… PTTTTNNNNNNGGGGG!" (Wild Bill Hickok → Wild Bill's stores + the door chime onomatopoeia).
**C. The editorial mutter:** "…for the record, I think the nickname Spike would be more appropriate." / "…that honestly, put me to sleep." Pure personality, no clue.

Winks appear on maybe a third of questions. A question with hook + lattice + wink is fully loaded — usually reserve full loading for openers and crowd-pleasers.

## 4. Pivot Phrasing Patterns

- Default: "what [category noun]" mid-sentence — *"was named after what insect, known for having 'supernatural powers'…"*
- The category noun sets answer SHAPE precisely: "what two-word term," "what one-word title," "what make and model," "what kind of animal." Precision here is the fairness contract — honor it (if you say two-word, the answer has two words).
- Front-loaded pivots for punchy questions: "What fast food company ran a contest in 2018…"
- Multi-answer control: "Please name either" / "name one of them" / "Name one of the other two." Never leave multiplicity implicit.

## 5. Connective Tissue

Ben's prose glue, so drafts read native: *"said [noun]"* as a back-reference ("said singer," "said mode of transportation" — use 1–2 per question max); *"chronicles"* for songs/books/films ("the song chronicles a woman's wish…"); *"known for / known to be / considered"* for reputational facts; *"would go on to / would eventually"* for narrative sequencing; sentence-initial *"Occurring in," "Located in," "Released in," "With albums like."* Numbers written naturally as spoken ("over 5,000 film trailers," "at just the age of 17").

## 6. Difficulty Engineering, Worked

Same anatomy, different loading:

**Weeknight-easy** (famous answer, three converging clues):
> What fast food company ran a contest in 2018, where they offered any family who named their baby Harland $11,000 dollars? → **KFC** (Harland = Colonel Sanders is nearly a giveaway; the fact carries the fun)

**Weeknight-medium** (familiar answer, one strong route + one riddle route):
> Sharing its name with a DC villain and originating from Bergamo, Italy, what is the name of a mischievous demon personified as a person that wore masks and colors? → **Harlequin**

**Championship-hard** (same moves, deeper cut, narrower pivot):
> The only surname to be shared by two men who have won the Best Actor award at the Oscars is what, with the two gentlemen winning in 1980/89, and 2005? → **Hoffman**
> What demeaning but comical nickname is shared by the Avatar after Roku, a top 3 NFL tight end all time, and a bronto-crane operator at the Slate Rock and Gravel company? → **Twinkletoes** *(championship questions often INVERT the lattice: give three routes, ask for the intersection — harder because no route is complete alone)*

**Bonus-tier list:** "For ten points each, name the five Best Picture winners with the most IMDB and Rotten Tomatoes ratings." Bounded, verifiable, scored per item.

## 7. Themed-Night Adaptation

Theme nights keep every mechanic; the constraint is that the ANSWER or primary route lives on-theme, while the bridge still leaves the theme (that's what keeps a Disney night winnable for non-Disney tables):
> (Villains night) One of the first "villains" we meet in SpongeBob is a new classmate who wants to beat SpongeBob up. Said character is what kind of fish, who shares its name with a Disney sidekick and an Animal House frat brother? → **Flounder** — villain-framed, but the routes are Disney + frat comedy.

Championship shows are theme-free but deeper; Michigan nights lean hardest on local anchors (the local anchor becomes the PRIMARY route, not the wink).

## 8. Recency & In-Memoriam

Openers tie to the week: "Last Friday, Chicago Cubs DH Justin Turner changed his walk-up song…", "In a hilarious reveal… this past week." Deaths get respectful-playful treatment — the person's work becomes the route, never the death itself as punchline. Swing round "off to a rough start" (2026 deaths) is the template: one item per person, each item a real trivia question routed THROUGH their legacy.

## 9. Scenario & Second-Person Questions

A recurring flavor — put the team inside the fact:
> Picture this… the year is 1969. You're walking down a street in the St. John's Wood district of London. You see four guys crossing the street in a straight line, and someone is taking their picture. What is the name of the street you're on? → **Abbey Road**
> Scenario: you and your soul mate want to take a cross-country road trip to see all 48 continental states, entering each state only once, ending at Santa Monica Pier… so you must start in what state? → **Maine**
"If you're [artist], and you're [inside their lyric]…" is the musical variant ("If you're Chad Kroeger staring at a photograph, where did you spend all the money you made at your job?" → **Arcade**). Use these to vary round texture — 0–2 per show.

## 10. Anti-Patterns — What Ben NEVER Does

- **No naked recall.** "What year did X happen?" / "Who wrote Y?" with no fact, no bridge, no fun — doesn't exist in 1,400 questions. Every question EARNS its answer with a story.
- **No obscure destinations.** The answer is never a thing the table has to be told about. If the answer needs a follow-up explanation, it was the setup all along.
- **No spelling-dependent or list-dump answers** for standalone questions (lists live in Bonus/PYL where they're scored per item).
- **No trick questions that punish correct knowledge.** Misdirects live in hooks and winks, never in the pivot. The category noun never lies.
- **No two questions in a round from the same domain-pair.** If R1 already bridged music×geography, the next music question bridges somewhere else.
- **No unbounded superlatives.** "One of the biggest…" appears only as color; the gradeable claim is always bounded ("the only," "the first," "since 2011," "according to [source]"). When a ranking is source-dependent, the source is named IN the question ("according to Vistaprint," "per Rolling Stone").
- **No pop-culture-free rounds and no pop-culture-only rounds.** Every round mixes registers: something old (history/geography), something now (this week), something for every table.
